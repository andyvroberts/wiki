# Glossary

### Admitted Market (US)
Each US state has local insurance companies.  No Lloyds syndicates participate in admitted lines today.  If the local market cannot place a risk, it can be designated as Surplus Lines business.

### Aviation (Class)

1.Physical Damage 
- Generally for Fixed Wing Aircraft (commercial & private), Gliders, Rotary aircraft, Microlights, Ballons - physical damage

2.Aviation Liability 

- Generally covers Passengers, other Third Parties and Products - liability

3.Loss of Licence/Use

- Generally covers losses resulting from unavailability of aircraft - loss

4.Airport Operators

- Gernally covers Airport Premises, Products and Hangers - physical damage, loss, liability

### Excess
The monetary amount deducted from each claim.  Usually, this amount is voluntary and results in a lower premium.

### Line of Busines (LOB)
A categorisation that groups insured risks.  Maybe for regulatory, geographic, risk assessment or other analytic purposes.  Less strictly defined then Class and not to be confused with the signed line of a Lineslip.  
Common examples include P&C (property and casualty) or CGL (commerical general liability).

### Marine (Class)

1.Hull & Yacht / Recreational Vessels

- Marine Hull - physical damage, liability
- Builders Risk - physical damage, liability

2.Loss of Earnings

- Generally covers business interrpution and loss of revenue - loss

3.Cargo & Goods in Transit

- Cargo (transit) - physical damage
- Stock Throughput (storage) - physical damage
- Jewlers Block - property, liability
- Specie - physical damage, loss
- Fine Art - physical damage, loss
- Satellite pre-launch - physical damage
- Cash in Transit - physical damage, loss
- Goods in Transit (land) - physical damage, loss, liability

4.War & Strkes  
- Generally covers marine war and stikes which includes terrorism - liability

5.Marine Liability

- Generally covers collision, Port authorities, Marinas and shipbuilders - liability

6.Political Risks Insurance (PRI)

- Generally covers actions by hostile governments - physical damage , loss

7.Offshore Energy

- Generally upstream energy exploration, construction and operation - physical damage, loss, liability

### Non-Marine (Class)

1.Property 

- Property (incl. machinery) - physical damage, loss
- Pecuniary - loss
- Construction All Risks (CAR) - physical damage, liability
- Erection All Risks (EAR) - physical damage, liability
- Onshore Energy (midstream / downstream) - physical damage, loss, liability (casualty)
- Cyber - loss

2.Business Interruption  
  
- Business Interruption - physical damage, loss
- Advanced Loss of Profits (ALOP) - physical damage, loss
- Delay in Start-Up (DSU) - physical damage, loss

3.Casualty (non-Marine Liability)

- Generally covers Employee, Public, Professional, Motor, Product and General liabilities. 

4.Bloodstock & Livestock

- Generally Horses to Farm animals - loss

5.Contingency 

- Generally losses arising from event cancellations, weather related loss, prize indemnity, and over redemption.

6.Personal Accident Insurance

- Generally accident, illness and death in service - loss

7.Kidnap & Ransom 

- Generally negotiation costs, medical costs and ransoms - loss

8.Malicious Tamper, Extortion, Product Recall

- General extortion, sabotage or defects - loss, liability

9.Intellectual Property Insurance

- Generally legal action for breaches of trademark or copyright - loss, liability

### Open Market
Traditional Broker led insurance placements.  Includes both individual and Subscription Business.

### Peril
An event that causes a loss, for example an explosion or a flood.  A Hazzard is a condition which effects or influences a Peril, for example storage of chemicals.  
Perils can be named in an insurance Policy, however a peril does not need to be explicitly stated for it to be Covered.

### Reinsurance (Class)

- Single Risk
- Multiple risks within a Class (e.g. Property, Marine Cargo)
- An Insurers entire Portfolio
- Catastrophe (natural/man-made) losses

### Subscription Business
The London Market is a subscription market. Multiple insurers can each share a percentage of a risk (depending on their available capacity).  They will receive that percentage of Premium and will be liable for that percentage of any claims.

### Surplus Lines (US)
Lloyds syndicates or insurance companies can conduct direct business within US states only after meeting certain criteria for non-local insurers.
